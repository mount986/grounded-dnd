# **Chapter 1: The World of the Backyard**

## **Setting Overview**

The campaign takes place entirely within a single suburban backyard — but at 1/1000th of human scale, it might as well be a continent. Towering blades of grass form dense forests. A garden hose becomes a river. The oak tree at the yard's center is a mountain. A rusted lawn chair is a ruin.

The four player characters have been shrunk by an unknown force connected to the BURG.L supercomputer — a mysterious AI research device buried somewhere in the yard. BURG.L is both an ally and an enigma: a source of quests, lore, and Raw Science chips, but tight-lipped about how and why the kids were shrunk.

The backyard is divided into biomes, each with distinct terrain, weather, bug populations, and resources. Players will explore them progressively as the campaign advances.

> **The Biomes of the Backyard** The Grasslands — Starting zone. Knee-high grass, clover patches, dandelions. Relatively safe. The Oak Tree — Mid-game hub. Massive root system, acorn deposits, spider territory above. The Hedge — Dense overgrown shrubs. Aphid herds, ladybugs, aggressive spiders. The Pond — Dangerous water biome. Mosquitoes, diving bell spiders, lily pad platforms. The Sandbox — Arid zone. Antlions, buried hazards, extreme heat in daytime. The Shed Wall — Late-game. Robotic lab creatures, BURG.L terminals, endgame content.


## **How 5e Is Modified**

Grounded uses the D&D 5e ruleset as a foundation with the following changes. All standard rules apply unless explicitly overridden in this book.

- **No spell slots:** Willow (the Druid) uses a modified ability system instead of traditional spellcasting. Her abilities use Spore Charges instead of spell slots. See her class entry for full details.
- **No levels:** Characters do not gain levels in the traditional sense. Instead, they spend Raw Science to purchase upgrades from their class upgrade tree. This means advancement is tied directly to in-game achievement rather than combat milestone.
- **No gold economy:** Crafting uses Bug Parts (tiered materials) rather than purchased equipment. See Chapter 3: Crafting for full details.
- **No random encounters:** All enemy appearances are scripted or DM-placed. The backyard feels like a living world, not a random dungeon.
- **Death saves work normally:** Standard 5e death save rules apply. At zero HP, a character falls unconscious and begins making death saving throws. The party can stabilize them or spend a healing action.

## **The Stat Block**

All four characters use the standard 5e stat block with the following ability scores and modifiers. Full pre-generated character sheets are provided in Chapter 2.

Ability scores range from 1–20 (higher = better). The modifier is (score − 10) ÷ 2, rounded down. All proficiency bonuses start at +2 and improve with upgrade milestones.

> **Proficiency Bonus Milestones** +2 Proficiency — Starting bonus for all characters. +3 Proficiency — Unlocked after spending 500 Raw Science total (cumulative across all upgrades). +4 Proficiency — Unlocked after spending 1,500 Raw Science total. +5 Proficiency — Unlocked after spending 3,000 Raw Science total. Unlike standard 5e, proficiency bonus increases are shared by the whole party simultaneously. This keeps the group balanced and rewards collective achievement.


## **Conditions**

The following conditions from 5e are used unchanged: Blinded, Charmed, Deafened, Frightened, Grappled, Incapacitated, Invisible, Paralyzed, Petrified, Poisoned, Prone, Restrained, Stunned, Unconscious.

The following Grounded-specific conditions are added:

- **Webbed:** Creature is Restrained. Can attempt a DC 13 Strength save at the start of each turn to break free. On a success, they are freed. On a failure, they remain Webbed and lose their movement.
- **Infected:** Creature has been exposed to fungal spores or diseased bug ichor. At the start of each turn, they take 1d4 poison damage. A DC 12 Constitution save at the end of each turn ends the condition on a success.
- **Drenched:** Creature has taken water damage or been soaked. Their movement speed is halved and they have disadvantage on fire-related attack rolls (if any) until they dry out (takes one full short rest or 10 minutes of dry weather).
- **Panicked:** Creature must use its movement to flee from the source of the Panicked condition. It cannot willingly move toward that source. Ends when the source is out of sight for one full round.

# **Chapter 3: Raw Science — The Progression System**

## **What Is Raw Science?**

Raw Science (RS) is the campaign's currency of advancement. It represents knowledge, data, and technological insight that the kids accumulate as they explore the yard, complete objectives, and overcome challenges. BURG.L uses Raw Science chips as a form of data storage, and collecting them is central to the game's story as well as character progression.

Raw Science is awarded by the DM for story achievements, exploration, and meaningful in-world accomplishments — not for killing bugs. Defeating enemies is sometimes necessary, but it is not inherently rewarded. This keeps the tone of the campaign on survival and ingenuity rather than combat grinding.

## **Awarding Raw Science**

The following table provides baseline Raw Science rewards. DMs should feel free to adjust values based on difficulty, player creativity, and pacing needs.

| **Achievement** | **Raw Science Awarded** | **Notes** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Discover a new biome | 50 RS | First visit only; revisits grant 0 |
| Complete a story objective | 100–300 RS | Scaled to objective difficulty (DM sets value) |
| Defeat a boss-tier bug | 200 RS | Named boss enemies only (see Bestiary) |
| Build a permanent structure | 25–75 RS | Awarded on completion, not per brick |
| Repair a damaged structure | 10 RS | Once per structure per session |
| Craft a Tier 2 item or higher | 20 RS per item | Requires Tinkerer (Pete) to craft; party shares reward |
| Rescue or protect an NPC | 50–150 RS | Varies by NPC importance |
| Solve an BURG.L quest | 75–200 RS | Scales with quest complexity |
| Identify a new creature type | 25 RS | First identification per creature species |
| Fully explore a zone | 100 RS | DM's discretion — all points of interest visited |

> **DM Guidance: When to Bonus Raw Science** Award bonus RS (25–50) when players: - Solve a problem creatively without combat when combat was expected - Use a character's unique class abilities in a particularly clever way - Make a sacrifice for the party (using the last healing charge, risking their character) - Deliver particularly good roleplaying moments consistent with their character - Navigate a biome hazard without taking damage Do not award RS for failed attempts. The yard is unforgiving.


## **Spending Raw Science — The Upgrade System**

Raw Science is spent at a BURG.L terminal (found in various locations across the yard) or during a long rest at the party's base if a BURG.L chip reader has been constructed there. All four characters share a single RS pool — the party decides together how to allocate upgrades.

Upgrades are purchased in tiers. You must unlock each tier before accessing the next. All tier costs are cumulative per character — buying a Tier 1 upgrade does not reduce the cost of the next Tier 1 upgrade; each is 100 RS.

| **Upgrade Tier** | **Raw Science Cost** | **Ability Improvement** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Tier 1 | 100 RS | Minor ability boost: +1 to attack rolls, save DC, or a skill check of choice |
| Tier 2 | 250 RS | Unlock a new feature use per rest, or improve a feature's die (e.g., d6 → d8) |
| Tier 3 | 500 RS | Add a new ability from a short list of class-specific options (see each class) |
| Tier 4 | 1,000 RS | Major upgrade: new subclass feature, or boost a core feature significantly |
| Tier 5 | 2,000 RS | Capstone unlock: signature ability unique to each character (see each class) |

## **Character-Specific Upgrade Options**

At Tier 3 and Tier 4, each character has a short list of class-specific options to choose from. These are listed on the individual character sheets in Appendix A. Examples include:

- **Max (Tier 3 options):** Improved Taunt (increase range from 20 to 40 feet), Fortified Stance (add CON modifier to AC while not moving), or Retaliatory Strike (when hit by a melee attack, Max can make one weapon attack as a reaction 1/round).
- **Willow (Tier 3 options):** Improved Spore Cloud (increase radius from 10 to 15 feet), Regrowth (at the start of each of her turns, Willow regains 1d4 HP if above 0 HP), or Spore Surge (one Spore Charge ability per long rest costs 0 charges).
- **Pete (Tier 3 options):** Extra Infusion (increase infusion count from 3 to 5), Emergency Build (Combat Build becomes a bonus action instead of an action, letting Pete build and attack in the same turn), or Reinforced Gadgets (all gadget HP and damage values doubled).
- **Hoops (Tier 3 options):** Improved Ambush Strike (extra damage increases from 2d6 to 3d6), Ghoststep (Hoops leaves no footprints and her movement makes no sound; Stealth checks have advantage in all terrain), or Triple Tap (Hoops can attack three times per Attack action with light ranged weapons only).

## **Party Upgrade Milestones**

The following milestones trigger when the party's total cumulative RS spend reaches these thresholds. These are automatic and apply to the whole party:

- **500 RS spent:** Proficiency bonus increases to +3. The party can access Tier 2 upgrade purchases.
- **1,000 RS spent:** All characters unlock their second Favored Terrain or Enemy (relevant classes). Base building unlocks the Tier 2 structure component list.
- **1,500 RS spent:** Proficiency bonus increases to +4. Tier 4 upgrades become available.
- **2,500 RS spent:** BURG.L unlocks the final story zone. Tier 5 (capstone) upgrades become available.
- **3,000 RS spent:** Proficiency bonus increases to +5. Full capstone abilities now active. Endgame content accessible.

> **DM Note: Pacing the Raw Science Economy** A well-paced campaign will reach 500 RS by the end of the second session, 1,000 RS around session 4-5, and 2,500 RS by session 8-10. Adjust award values if the party is advancing too quickly (slow the story) or too slowly (players may feel underpowered). Never award RS for things the players didn't earn. The scarcity is part of what makes each upgrade feel meaningful. A Tier 5 capstone should feel like a hard-won victory.


# **Chapter 4: Resting & Recovery**

## **Short Rest**

A short rest is at least 1 hour of light activity (eating, tending wounds, scouting from a safe position). Characters can spend Hit Dice to recover HP. Each character rolls their Hit Die and adds their Constitution modifier. They can spend as many Hit Dice as they have remaining.

Willow regains 1d4 Spore Charges during a short rest if resting near fungal terrain. Pete's infused items persist through short rests but not long rests.

## **Long Rest**

A long rest requires at least 8 hours of low-activity time, and must be taken at a safe location. The party's base counts as safe once it has at least one wall structure and a roof. An undefended location is not safe if bugs are within 100 feet.

During a long rest: all HP is restored, all Hit Dice are regained (up to max), all ability recharges reset, Willow regains all Spore Charges, Pete regains all Infusions and Gadgets.

A long rest is also the time when Pete can construct or repair Lego structures (see Chapter 5: The Building System).

> **Disrupted Rests** If the party is attacked during a long rest, the rest is disrupted. They recover only as if they had taken a short rest, regardless of how much time passed. Proper base defenses — walls, spike traps, watch routines — can prevent rests from being disrupted. This is a core incentive for building. A well-defended base means safe long rests. An undefended camp means interrupted recovery and cumulative exhaustion.


## **Exhaustion**

Standard 5e exhaustion rules apply. The party can gain exhaustion from going too long without a safe long rest, from environmental hazards (extreme heat in the Sandbox, cold wet conditions near the Pond), or from specific enemy abilities.

Exhaustion has 6 levels. Level 1 causes disadvantage on ability checks. Level 3 causes disadvantage on attack rolls and saves. Level 6 causes death. Each long rest removes one level of exhaustion.

# **Chapter 5: The Building System**

One of the most distinctive features of this campaign is that the players build actual physical structures at the table using real bricks. These structures exist simultaneously in the story world and on the game surface — they protect resources, channel enemy movement, create tactical advantages, and reflect the party's investment in their base.

This chapter covers the full building system: how Bricks are harvested and processed, how structures are built and sized, how durability and damage work, how bug raids target and damage structures, and how structures can be repaired or torn down.

> **What Are Bricks?** Bricks are the real-world building pieces used to construct in-game structures. In the world of the backyard, they represent whatever material the kids can find and shape — dried grass, bark chips, pebbles, plastic fragments, hardened sap, and more. The material is purely flavor: mechanically, a Brick is a Brick regardless of where it was harvested. Bricks are a shared party resource tracked on a single pool. Every Brick harvested goes into the party's inventory. Pete converts raw material into usable Bricks (see Harvesting below). Bricks are finite and decisions about where to spend them matter.


## **Harvesting Bricks**

Bricks cannot be harvested during combat. They require a moment of focused scavenging in a non-hostile environment. The process is split between the party gathering raw material and Pete processing it into usable Bricks.

### **Step 1: The Harvest Action**

Any character can take a Harvest Action when the party is not in combat and is in a harvestable environment. This is a dedicated in-game action that takes approximately 10 minutes of in-world time.

- **Each character can take one Harvest Action per short rest and up to three Harvest Actions per long rest.:**
- **When taking a Harvest Action, the player rolls the appropriate Harvest Die for their current biome (see table below) and adds the result to the party's raw material count.:**
- **Multiple characters can harvest simultaneously. All results are added together before Pete processes them.:**
- **Pete can also take Harvest Actions. When Pete harvests, he adds his Intelligence modifier to the die roll (minimum +1).:**

| **Biome / Environment** | **Harvest Die** | **Flavor Description** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Grasslands (starting zone) | 1d6 | Dried grass stalks, clover stems, dandelion fluff — plentiful but light |
| The Oak Tree area | 1d8 | Bark chips, acorn cap fragments, hardened sap — denser and more workable |
| The Hedge | 1d8 | Twigs, leaf ribs, berry husks — irregular but abundant in the undergrowth |
| The Pond / Water's Edge | 1d4 | Reed sections, mud-hardened clay, waterlogged debris — sparse and wet |
| The Sandbox | 1d6 | Compacted sand clusters, plastic fragment shards, bottle cap pieces |
| The Shed Wall / Lab area | 1d10 | Synthetic debris, insulation foam chunks, hardened adhesive — best yield |
| Player's Base (scavenging nearby) | 1d4 | Remnants and offcuts from previous builds — lean pickings |

> **DM Note: Refreshing Harvest Actions** Each character's Harvest Action allotment resets at rests — 1 recovers on a short rest, all 3 recover on a long rest. This means a well-rested party exploring a new biome can harvest 3 actions each (12 total for the full party of 4) before their resource starts depleting. A party that skips rests will find themselves brick-poor. Track each character's remaining harvest actions on their character sheet. Suggestion: use a simple tally in the margin — 3 boxes, cross them off as used.


### **Step 2: Pete's Conversion**

Raw material gathered by the party cannot be used directly. Pete must spend time processing it into workable Bricks. This represents him sorting, shaping, and sizing the material to be reliably stackable and structurally useful.

- **Pete can convert raw material during any rest (short or long).:**
- **Conversion ratio: every 1 unit of raw material becomes 1 Brick. There is no waste in conversion — Pete is efficient.:**
- **Pete can convert any amount of raw material in a single rest period. There is no cap on how much he can process per rest.:**
- **Raw material that has not been converted by Pete cannot be used in building. It sits in the party's inventory until Pete processes it.:**
- **If Pete is incapacitated, unconscious, or absent during a rest, no conversion takes place that rest.:**

> **Design Intent: Why the Two-Step?** Separating harvesting from conversion does two things. First, it makes Pete mechanically essential even when he isn't fighting — his presence at rest is what turns raw effort into usable material. Second, it creates a natural throttle: the party can harvest aggressively but Pete is always the processing bottleneck. If he's burned out, busy with repairs, or focused on crafting, the brick supply stalls. This generates real decisions.


## **Building Structures**

When the party builds, the players physically construct a structure using their real bricks on the game surface. The in-game cost and rules are determined by the size and complexity of what they have built.

### **When Can the Party Build?**

- **During a long rest:** This is the primary building window. Pete directs construction and can build or upgrade one structure per long rest as part of his rest activities (sleeping counts as partial rest; building takes the remaining time). Other party members can assist, granting Pete advantage on any relevant checks.
- **During a short rest:** Pete can make minor additions or repairs but cannot construct a new full structure. He can complete a Combat Build deployment (see below) or perform a Rapid Repair.
- **Emergency builds in combat:** Pete can use his Combat Build ability as a standard action at any time during combat, with no limit on uses. There is no rest requirement — Pete is limited only by the action economy. The Field Engineer subclass upgrade improves this to a bonus action (see below).

### **Determining Brick Cost**

The Brick cost of a structure is proportional to its physical size on the table. The DM determines cost by judging the finished structure against the following baseline:

> **The Brick Sizing Baseline** 1 Brick = one standard brick unit (approximately 1x2 stud size in physical play). A structure's cost is equal to the approximate number of bricks used to build it, counted after construction. The DM has final say and should eyeball generously — the goal is proportionality, not a precise brick census. The Blueprint table below gives reference costs for common structure types. Use these as anchors when judging player-designed structures.


| **Structure Type** | **Base Brick Cost** | **Durability Tier** | **Notes** |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Wall section (short) | 4 Bricks | Fragile | Roughly hand-height; blocks movement and provides cover |
| Wall section (tall) | 8 Bricks | Fragile | Full-height wall; provides full cover from one direction |
| Reinforced wall (short) | 4 Bricks + Sturdy upgrade | Sturdy | Apply Reinforcement during Pete's long rest construction |
| Reinforced wall (tall) | 8 Bricks + Sturdy upgrade | Sturdy | As above; counts as fortification for raid resistance |
| Floor / platform section | 6 Bricks | Sturdy | Elevated platform; bugs must climb or be boosted to reach |
| Roof section | 6 Bricks | Fragile | Prevents aerial attack; reduces weather effects inside base |
| Gate / door frame | 5 Bricks | Fragile | Passable by party; closed gate blocks bug movement |
| Tower / elevated post | 10 Bricks | Sturdy | Grants Hoops or other ranged characters high ground bonus (+1 to hit) |
| Spike barrier (external) | 3 Bricks | Fragile | Deals 1d4 damage to any bug that moves through it; destroyed after 3 hits |

| BURG.L terminal housing | 15 Bricks | Reinforced | Protects a BURG.L terminal; required for safe long-rest RS spending |

### **DM Boons and Penalties**

Because players design their own structures, the DM may award boons or impose penalties based on the quality and cleverness of the physical design. This is intentional — it rewards creative building.

- **Boon: Smart design:** A structure that incorporates a natural chokepoint, interlocks with terrain, or creates a clearly advantageous firing position may receive a Durability tier upgrade at no extra Brick cost (e.g., a Fragile wall becomes Sturdy if it perfectly seals a passage).
- **Boon: Efficient construction:** A compact, tight, well-supported design may cost 1–2 fewer Bricks than the DM's initial estimate.
- **Penalty: Unstable design:** A top-heavy, poorly supported, or physically precarious structure is also precarious in-game. The DM may reduce its HP by up to one-third or rule that a single heavy hit knocks it Prone (toppled), requiring re-erection before it grants cover again.
- **Penalty: Overreach:** A structure that tries to do too much — long wall with no supports, roof with no walls, tower with a single-brick base — may be ruled as Fragile regardless of intended tier, or may require additional Bricks to brace before it grants its full stats.

## **Structure Durability & HP**

All structures have two health-related stats: Hit Points (HP) and a Durability Tier. HP tracks how much damage a structure has taken. The Durability Tier determines how much damage the structure can absorb before any HP is lost at all — the damage threshold.

Damage below the threshold is shrugged off. Only hits that meet or exceed the threshold deal HP damage. This models the difference between a bug gnawing ineffectually at a reinforced wall versus a large creature slamming into a flimsy grass barricade.

| **FRAGILE** AC 10 | Threshold 3 | **HP Range:** 5–15 HP (DM sets based on size; short wall = 5 HP, tall wall = 10 HP, roof = 8 HP) **Damage threshold:** Any hit dealing 3 or more damage bypasses and deals full HP damage. Hits dealing 1–2 damage are absorbed. **Example structures:** Improvised barriers, basic grass walls, quick shelters, spike strips **Raid behavior:** Bugs will attack Fragile structures freely. They do not need to make a targeting decision — Fragile structures are obvious weak points. |
| --- | --- |

| **STURDY** AC 13 | Threshold 6 | **HP Range:** 20–40 HP (short reinforced wall = 20 HP, tall = 35 HP, floor platform = 25 HP, tower = 40 HP) **Damage threshold:** Any hit dealing 6 or more damage bypasses and deals full HP damage. Hits dealing 1–5 damage are absorbed. **Example structures:** Reinforced bark walls, elevated platforms, gated entrances, resource vaults **Raid behavior:** Bugs prefer Fragile structures first. They will only target Sturdy structures if no Fragile options remain in their path, or if their raid objective is the Sturdy structure specifically. |
| --- | --- |

| **REINFORCED** AC 16 | Threshold 10 | **HP Range:** 50–80 HP (resource vault = 60 HP, BURG.L terminal housing = 75 HP, full base wall = 50 HP) **Damage threshold:** Only hits dealing 10 or more damage bypass the threshold. Virtually immune to small bugs. **Example structures:** BURG.L terminal housing, full resource vaults, late-game fortifications **Raid behavior:** Only boss-tier enemies and siege events will deliberately target Reinforced structures. Standard bugs will avoid them entirely unless all other options are gone. |
| --- | --- |

> **Tracking HP at the Table** Each structure should have a small notecard placed next to it on the table listing: - Structure name / type - Current HP / Max HP - Durability Tier (F / S / R) - Any active upgrades (Sap Seal, Grass Weave Lashing, etc.) When a structure takes damage, the DM (or a designated player) updates the card. When a structure reaches 0 HP, physically remove or topple it on the table. Destroyed structures leave a rubble footprint — the area counts as difficult terrain until cleared. Clearing rubble takes a short rest and yields half the original Brick cost in salvageable material (before recycling rolls).


## **Repairing Structures**

Damaged structures can be repaired. Only Pete can perform full structural repairs; other characters can stabilize a structure (stopping it from getting worse) but cannot restore HP.

- **Rapid Repair (Pete, Action):** Pete restores 2d6 + INT modifier HP to one adjacent structure. Requires 1 Bug Part of any tier as repair material. Can be used during short rests or outside of combat. Cannot be used in combat except via the Field Engineer subclass.
- **Full Repair (Pete, Long Rest):** Pete fully restores one structure to max HP during a long rest. This uses his build action for that rest — he cannot also construct a new structure in the same long rest. Costs 1 Brick per 10 HP restored (rounded up).
- **Stabilize (Any character, Action):** Any character adjacent to a structure at 5 HP or fewer can spend their action to shore it up, preventing it from taking further damage until Pete can repair it. The structure cannot be further damaged until the start of that character's next turn. This is a stalling action only — it does not restore HP.
- **Tier Downgrade on Destruction:** A structure that reaches 0 HP and is rebuilt from rubble (rather than repaired before destruction) is rebuilt at one Durability Tier lower than its original. A Sturdy wall rebuilt from rubble is Fragile. Pete can re-upgrade it, but this costs a second long rest build action.

## **Tearing Down & Recycling Bricks**

The party can choose to dismantle a standing structure and recover its Bricks for use elsewhere. This is useful when the party moves to a new biome and wants to relocate their base, or when they want to redesign their defenses.

- **Teardown time:** Tearing down a structure takes 10 minutes of in-world time per structure section. Pete must supervise; other characters can assist but cannot tear down without him.
- **Recovery roll:** After teardown, roll percentile dice (d100). The result, with a minimum floor of 50, determines the percentage of Bricks recovered. On a roll of 01–49, treat the result as 50. On a roll of 50–100, use the actual result. A roll of 100 recovers all Bricks (rare — materials aligned perfectly).
- **Half the structure's Bricks are always guaranteed:** Even on the worst possible outcome, the party recovers at least half. The remainder represents material too damaged, warped, or broken to reuse.
- **Damaged structures recover fewer Bricks:** A structure at 50% HP or less recovers Bricks at half the rolled percentage (minimum 25%). A structure at 0 HP (rubble) can still be salvaged but recovers a flat 1d4 Bricks regardless of original cost.

> **Recycling Example** The party built a tall reinforced wall (8 Bricks) near the Grasslands. They now want to move it to the Oak Tree area. Pete supervises teardown (10 min). The DM rolls d100: 63. 63% of 8 = 5.04, rounded to 5 Bricks recovered. 3 Bricks are lost to the teardown. If the wall had been at 40% HP (damaged), the recovery is halved: 63% x 50% = ~31%, minimum 25%, so 2 Bricks recovered. Damaged structures are expensive to relocate. This creates a real decision: repair before moving, or accept the material loss?


## **Bug Raids: When Structures Are Attacked**

Bugs in the Grounded world are not mindlessly aggressive — but they are territorial, hungry, and will respond to threats. When the party disturbs the yard's ecosystem, structures near their base become targets.

Raids are always DM-initiated. They are never random. The DM uses raids as a pacing tool — to create tension, punish overextension, or drive the story forward. The three raid tiers are:

| **Raid Intensity** | **Trigger** | **Raid Behavior** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Skirmish | Party disturbs a nearby nest or kills 3+ bugs in one session | 1d4 bugs attack the nearest external structure. No boss present. Lasts 2 rounds. |
| Raid | Party enters a new biome aggressively, or a story beat triggers it | 2d4 bugs attack in waves over 4 rounds. One structure is targeted per wave. |
| Siege | Story-driven event (DM-placed). Cannot be triggered accidentally. | Sustained multi-wave assault. Bugs target the Resource Vault and BURG.L housing first. Boss may be present. |

### **Raid Targeting Priority**

Bugs in a raid are not smart, but they are instinctual. They target structures in the following priority order:

- **Spike barriers and external traps** — Bugs try to clear the path first. They will destroy these before advancing.
- **Fragile walls blocking their path** — Any Fragile structure between the bugs and their objective is attacked first.
- **Resource Vault** — If accessible, bugs are drawn to the scent of stored materials.
- **BURG.L terminal housing** — In siege events only. Story-driven; the bugs seem guided toward it.
- **Party members** — Bugs that reach the interior of the base will attack the party. Max's Taunt can redirect them.

### **Structure Attack Rolls**

Bugs attacking structures use the following simplified attack rules:

- **Attack roll:** Bug makes a melee attack roll against the structure's AC (10 for Fragile, 13 for Sturdy, 16 for Reinforced). On a hit, roll the bug's damage dice normally.
- **Damage threshold:** Apply the threshold before subtracting HP. A bug dealing 4 damage against a Sturdy wall (threshold 6) deals 0 HP damage. A bug dealing 7 damage against the same wall deals 7 HP damage.
- **Critical hits on structures:** On a natural 20, bugs deal maximum damage on their damage dice (no extra dice). This represents a particularly well-placed blow finding a structural weakness.
- **Area damage:** Some boss-tier bugs and siege events have area attacks. These hit all structures in a radius for half damage, ignoring thresholds. These are listed in the Bestiary.

## **Structure Upgrades**

Pete can apply upgrades to structures beyond their base stats. Upgrades represent additional reinforcement, clever modifications, or special materials applied during construction or maintenance.

| **Upgrade** | **Effect** |
| --- | --- |
| Sturdy Reinforcement (Pete action, 1/structure/long rest) | Upgrades any Fragile structure to Sturdy. Costs 0 extra Bricks but requires Pete to spend his long rest build action on it. |
| Reinforced Shell (Pete action, 1/structure/long rest) | Upgrades any Sturdy structure to Reinforced. Costs 2 additional Bricks. Pete must have the Tier 2 Field Engineer upgrade to perform this. |
| Sap Seal (consumable, 1 Bug Part Tier 1) | Applied to any structure. Until the next long rest, that structure's damage threshold increases by 2 (e.g. Fragile threshold goes from 3 to 5). |
| Grass Weave Lashing (Pete, 10 min) | Binds two adjacent wall sections together. Neither can be knocked Prone (toppled) by creature impact separately — both must be destroyed first. |
| Elevated Anchor (Pete, action) | Pins a floor or roof section to two supporting walls. Aerial attackers can no longer dislodge the roof as a separate target; it must be destroyed through the walls first. |
| Camouflage Netting (Pete, 10 min, 1 Bug Part) | Applied to any structure. Bugs making raid decisions treat that structure as lower priority (effectively invisible to raid targeting unless it is the only remaining structure). |

## **Combat Builds**

Pete can deploy small improvised structures in the middle of combat as a standard action — no upgrade or rest required. These Combat Builds are mechanically distinct from base structures: they are smaller, weaker, and temporary, but immediately useful for shaping the battlefield. The Field Engineer subclass upgrade (available at Tier 3) converts Combat Build from an action into a bonus action, allowing Pete to build and still attack or use another ability in the same turn.

> **Combat Build Rules Summary** WHEN: Pete's action. No rest required, no limit on uses per combat. WHERE: One square adjacent to Pete at the time of placement. COST: No Brick cost. These use on-hand debris, not the party's Brick stockpile. DURATION: Persist until destroyed or until the end of the encounter (whichever comes first). Exception: Sap Traps persist for 1 minute (10 rounds) after combat ends. OPTIONS: Pebble Barrier — 5 HP, Fragile, 1/4 cover to all creatures on one side. Spike Strip    — 0 HP (not a cover structure). Any creature entering the square takes 1d6 piercing damage. Destroyed after dealing damage 3 times. Sap Trap       — 0 HP. Square becomes difficult terrain. Creatures entering make a DC 13 DEX save or are Restrained for one round. FIELD ENGINEER UPGRADE (Tier 3): Combat Build becomes a bonus action, freeing Pete's action for attacks or other abilities.


Combat Builds do not count as base structures and cannot be repaired. When an encounter ends, remove all Combat Build pieces from the table. If the party wishes to convert a Combat Build into a permanent structure, Pete must spend a full short rest reinforcing it, at which point it costs the appropriate Brick total and receives the Fragile Durability tier.

The following configurations give new DMs a reference point for what a party's base might look like at different points in the campaign. These are not blueprints — the players build what they want. These are only examples of reasonable defensive postures.

> **DM Tip: Let the Base Tell the Story** The physical state of the party's base is a record of their campaign. Encourage players to name their structures, note which bug destroyed which wall, and feel ownership over what they have built. A base that has never been attacked is boring. A base that has been half-destroyed and rebuilt stronger is a story. Use raids to make the base feel alive and worth defending.

# **Chapter 6: Crafting**

In the backyard, survival depends on what you can make. The kids cannot walk into a store or buy better gear — they must harvest the raw materials the yard provides and turn them into weapons, armor, and tools. Crafting is Willow's domain. Her deep understanding of natural materials, bug biology, and organic chemistry makes her uniquely suited to work with what the yard gives them.

This chapter covers Bug Parts (the crafting currency), Willow's crafter abilities and progression, and full tables of craftable weapons and armor drawn directly from the world of Grounded.

## **Willow's Crafter Abilities**

In addition to her Mycologist class abilities, Willow gains a set of Crafter abilities that give her sole access to the crafting system. No other character can craft weapons, armor, or consumables — only Willow. Other party members can assist (granting advantage on crafting checks), but Willow must direct and perform all crafting work.

This is intentional: just as Pete is the indispensable builder, Willow is the indispensable maker. Her presence at rest is what keeps the party's gear current with the threats they face.

> **Crafter Ability: Naturalist's Workshop** Willow can spend time during any rest crafting items from the party's Bug Part stockpile. Crafting takes 30 minutes of in-world time per item (reduced to 15 minutes at Rank 3). Willow can craft multiple items in a single long rest, up to a number equal to her Wisdom modifier + her current Crafting Rank (e.g. Rank 2 + WIS +3 = 5 items per rest). Crafting requires no ability check for standard items at or below Willow's current Rank. Crafting an item exactly one tier above her current Rank is possible but requires a DC 15 Wisdom (Nature) check. On a failure, the materials are consumed and the item is not produced. She cannot attempt items two or more tiers above her current Rank.


### **Crafter Rank — Unlocking Higher Tiers**

Willow's crafting ability is gated by her Crafter Rank, which is upgraded by spending Raw Science at a BURG.L terminal. Raw Science spent on Crafter Rank upgrades comes from the party's shared pool and counts toward the party's total RS spend milestone.

| **Crafting Rank** | **RS Cost (cumulative)** | **Unlocks** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Rank 1 — Scavenger | Free (starting rank) | Can craft all Tier 1 weapons, armor, and consumables. Can collect T1 Bug Parts. |
| Rank 2 — Naturalist | 150 RS | Can craft all Tier 2 weapons and armor. Can collect T2 Bug Parts. Unlocks Repair action. |
| Rank 3 — Artisan | 400 RS | Can craft all Tier 3 weapons and armor. Can collect T3 Bug Parts. Unlocks Masterwork feature and Elemental Infusion (5 types). |
| Rank 4 — Forgemaster | 900 RS | Can craft boss-tier (T4) equipment. Unlocks Batch Craft, Venomcraft, and 2 premium Elemental Infusion types (Explosive, Fungal). |

> **Crafter Rank vs. Class Upgrades** Crafter Rank upgrades are separate from Willow's standard class upgrade tree. Spending RS on Crafter Rank does not count against her class Tier purchases, and vice versa. Both draw from the same shared party RS pool, so the party must decide how to allocate resources between combat abilities and crafting capability. This is intentional tension.


### **Crafter Rank 3 — Masterwork**

At Rank 3, Willow unlocks the Masterwork feature. Once per long rest, she can designate one item she crafts as a Masterwork. A Masterwork item gains one of the following bonuses (Willow’s choice at time of crafting):
- **Sharp:** Weapon gains +1 to attack rolls.
- **Heavy:** Weapon’s damage die increases by one step (e.g. d6 becomes d8).
- **Lightweight:** Armor does not impose disadvantage on Stealth checks, regardless of type.
- **Tough:** Armor gains +1 AC beyond its listed value.
- **Balanced:** Weapon gains the Thrown property (range 20/60) if it didn’t have it, or increases Thrown range by 20 feet if it did.

### **Crafter Rank 3 — Elemental Infusion**

At Rank 3, Willow can infuse a weapon with an elemental property at the time of crafting. The infusion type is chosen when the weapon is made and cannot be changed afterward. An infused weapon deals bonus damage of the chosen type on a hit, plus a unique side effect. Only one infusion can be applied per weapon. Five infusion types unlock at Rank 3; two more powerful types unlock at Rank 4.
Elemental infusions deal +1d4 damage of the listed type on a hit (once per turn). The side effect triggers on the same hit and lasts until the end of the target’s next turn unless otherwise stated. Infusion effects can only trigger once per target per combat.
**Venom (Poison). **+1d4 poison. DC 13 CON save or Poisoned until end of target’s next turn. Cost: +1 T2 part. Sources: spider fangs, stinkbug glands, ant acid.
**Spicy (Fire). **+1d4 fire. Target loses its reaction until start of its next turn. Cost: +1 T2 part. Sources: fire ant glands, spicy coaltana residue, chili debris.
**Mint (Cold). **+1d4 cold. Target’s movement speed reduced by 10ft until end of its next turn. Cost: +1 T2 part. Sources: mint shard, ice cap mushroom residue.
**Sour (Acid). **+1d4 acid. Target’s AC reduced by 1 until end of its next turn (armor-eating). Cost: +1 T2 part. Sources: aphid honeydew, ant acid, sour staff residue.
**Salt (Necrotic). **+1d4 necrotic. Target cannot regain HP until the start of its next turn. Cost: +1 T2 part. Sources: salt crystals from potato chip debris, slug residue.

### **Crafter Rank 4 — Venomcraft (Premium Infusions)**

At Rank 4, Willow unlocks two premium infusion types that deal +1d6 damage (instead of +1d4) and carry more powerful effects. These cost more Bug Parts to apply and represent the pinnacle of Willow’s craft.
**Explosive (Fire + Thunder). **+1d6 fire on hit. All creatures within 5ft of the target take 1d4 fire damage (DEX DC 13 to halve); those who fail are knocked Prone. Cost: +2 T3 parts. Sources: bombardier beetle glands, pinecone resin.
**Fungal (Poison — Spore). **+1d6 poison on hit. Target makes DC 14 CON save or gains the Infected condition (takes 1d4 poison damage at the start of each of its turns until it succeeds on a DC 12 CON save at the end of its turn). Cost: +2 T3 parts. Sources: shroom spores, infected mushroom caps.

### **Crafter Rank 4 — Batch Craft**

At Rank 4, Willow can craft consumables in batches of 3 for the cost of 2 (round down on parts). This applies to all consumables (bandages, antidotes, food items) but not to weapons or armor.

## **Bug Parts — The Crafting Currency**

Bug Parts are the raw materials harvested from defeated enemies. Unlike Bricks, Bug Parts do not require any processing — they are immediately usable once harvested. Any character can collect Bug Parts from a defeated creature, but only parts at or below Willow's current Crafter Rank can be collected and stored.

Bug Parts are a shared party resource tracked as three separate pools: T1, T2, and T3 (plus T4 for boss remnants). Each pool is tracked independently on the party resource sheet.

| **Tier** | **Part Name** | **Primary Sources** | **Flavor** |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| T1 — Soft | Soft Fiber, Silk, Chitin Flake | Gnats, Aphids, Weevils, Mites, most flying insects | Flexible, lightweight. Good for basic armor and tools. |
| T2 — Hard | Hardened Chitin, Carapace Chunk, Stinger Shard | Ants, Beetles, Bees, Stinkbugs, Wolf Spiders | Dense and durable. Needed for mid-tier weapons and reinforced armor. |
| T3 — Reinforced | Reinforced Shell, Venom Gland, Compound Eye | Orb Weavers, Bombardier Beetles, Ladybirds, Mantis | The toughest natural materials in the yard. Required for late-game gear. |

> **Harvesting Bug Parts** After a combat encounter, any character can spend an action harvesting Bug Parts from defeated creatures before the party moves on. Harvesting takes approximately 5 minutes per creature (in-world time). The DM determines yield: Small/weak bugs (gnats, mites, aphids):  1d2 T1 parts Medium bugs (ants, beetles, stinkbugs):   1d4 T2 parts (or 1d6 T1 if T2 not unlocked) Large bugs (spiders, mantis, bees):       1d4 T3 parts + 1d4 T2 parts Boss creatures:                            1 T4 Boss Remnant (guaranteed) + 2d6 T3 parts Parts above Willow's current Crafter Rank cannot be harvested — the party lacks the knowledge to extract them usefully. They are left behind.

## **Crafting Procedures & Rules**

### **When Can Willow Craft?**

- **During a long rest:** Primary crafting window. Willow can craft up to WIS modifier + Crafting Rank items. Each takes 30 minutes of in-world time (15 min at Rank 3). Other party members can assist one item per rest, granting advantage on the crafting check if one is required.
- **During a short rest:** Willow can craft one consumable only during a short rest. No weapons or armor.
- **In combat:** Willow cannot craft during combat. Her Combat Build equivalent is using existing consumables, not making new ones.

### **Crafting Checks**

Most crafting at or below Willow's Rank requires no check — she succeeds automatically. A check is only required in the following situations:

- **One tier above Rank:** DC 15 Wisdom (Nature). On failure: materials are consumed, item not produced.
- **Masterwork designation:** DC 13 Wisdom (Nature). On failure: item is crafted normally without the Masterwork bonus. Materials are not lost.
- **Elemental Infusion:** No check required at any Rank. Choosing an infusion type always succeeds. Only the underlying craft check (if above Rank) can fail.
- **Crafting under duress:** If the party is in a dangerous or pressured situation (fleeing, partially surrounded, during a disrupted rest), the DM may impose disadvantage on any crafting checks.

### **Parts Consumption on Failure**

If a crafting check fails and the item is not produced, the Bug Parts used are fully consumed. This is intentional — failed attempts represent wasted materials, spoiled reagents, or a design that didn't hold together. It creates meaningful consequences for attempting above-Rank crafts.

> **DM Guidance: Managing the Crafting Economy** Bug Parts should be reasonably plentiful in zones appropriate to the party's level, but never so abundant that Willow can craft everything on the table. The party will need to make decisions: do they craft more weapons, more armor, or stockpile consumables? A useful guideline: after a medium-difficulty combat encounter, the party should earn enough Bug Parts to craft 1-2 items of the appropriate tier. After a boss encounter, they should earn enough T3 parts for a complete armor set or 2-3 weapons. If the party is consistently running out of parts, increase drop yields slightly. If Willow is crafting every item on the table by session 3, reduce yields. The sweet spot is that crafting feels impactful but not trivial.


### **Equipment Slots**

Each character has a fixed number of equipment slots:

- **Weapon slots:** 2 (one primary weapon + one sidearm). A two-handed weapon occupies both slots.
- **Armor slots:** 3 (head, body, legs — or full set for sets that occupy all three).
- **Shield slot:** 1 (separate from weapon slots; replaces off-hand weapon).
- **Consumable slots:** 4 (belt-accessible items). Additional consumables must be stored in the party's base or camp inventory and cannot be accessed during combat.
